# Communication Polish

Writing that works reads like someone thinking clearly out loud. The words land in rhythm. The meaning arrives without friction. The reader never re-reads a sentence because the writer lost the thread.

This skill rewrites, polishes, and critiques text across any register — formal, casual, technical, personal — preserving the author's voice while sharpening everything around it.

## Three-Pass Workflow

Every piece of text runs through three passes. No skipping.

### Pass 1 — Preserve Intent and Facts

Read the original. Identify what the author is actually saying and what they want the reader to do, feel, or know afterward. Lock those down. Nothing in the next two passes changes the meaning or removes a fact the author included deliberately.

If the intent is ambiguous, state what you interpreted before rewriting. Never silently redirect someone's meaning.

Checklist:
- Claims stay attached to the author's source intent
- Names, dates, figures, commitments, and constraints remain unchanged unless the author explicitly asks to reframe
- The author's core stance and decision survive intact — never invert argument direction
- Emotional register stays in the author's lane, not yours

### Pass 2 — Rhythm, Cadence, and Flow

This is where the craft lives.

**Sentence rhythm.** Vary length. A long sentence that builds momentum earns its weight when a short one lands after it. Three sentences of identical length in a row flatline the reader's attention.

**Read-aloud flow.** If a sentence stumbles when spoken in the reader's head, it fails. Hard consonant clusters at transition points, buried subjects, and dangling modifiers break the internal voice. Read every rewrite aloud in your head. If you trip, revise.

**Transitions.** Every sentence should feel like it belongs after the one before it. If you can swap two paragraphs without the reader noticing, neither paragraph is earning its spot.

**Active voice by default.** Passive is a tool, not a habit. Use it when the receiver of the action matters more than the actor. Otherwise, name the subject and let them act.

**Eliminate the dead.** Cut filler phrases ("it should be noted that", "in order to", "the fact that"), redundant qualifiers ("very unique", "completely finished"), and throat-clearing openers ("I think that", "I just wanted to"). Every word earns its seat or gets cut.

**Stress and emphasis.** English stress patterns put weight at the end of a clause. "The project failed because nobody tested it" hits harder than "Nobody tested it, so the project failed." End on the word that matters.

**Paragraph rhythm.** Each paragraph needs internal momentum — a mix of sentence lengths that builds to a point. Check for consecutive sentences starting with the same word; three "The" openers in a row flatline attention. Every paragraph should contain at least one sentence strong enough to stand alone.

**Parallel structure.** When listing or comparing, keep the grammatical shape consistent. Parallel lines create rhythm. Broken parallelism creates stumble.

### Pass 3 — Tone Policy

Apply the appropriate register. The default is: **authentic, grounded, genuine, kind.** The writing sounds like a real person who respects the reader and has something worth saying.

Kind doesn't mean soft. Kind means you care enough to be clear.

**Tone escalation (when the writing or idea needs it):**

1. **Firm** — "This section doesn't land. Here's why and what fixes it."
2. **Firmer** — "This argument has a structural problem. The premise at [X] doesn't support the conclusion at [Y]. Rework needed."
3. **Blunt with kindness** — "This idea doesn't work. Not the phrasing — the idea itself. [Specific reason.] Here's what a stronger version looks like."

Blunt never means cruel. The goal is always to hand the person something better, not to make them feel worse. Directness is respect — it means you trust them to handle honest feedback. For the full escalation framework with detailed examples at each level, see the Directness Ladder in `references/writing-framework.md`.

**Register matching.** Read the original's formality level and match it. "Hey, quick update" should not become "I wanted to take a moment to provide a brief update." A casual email stays casual. A formal report stays formal. Only shift register if the author asks.

## Direct Feedback Template

When critiquing (not just polishing), structure feedback as:

**What works.** Name the strongest element. Be specific — "The opening line sets the stakes immediately" beats "good intro."

**What breaks.** Identify the weakest structural element. Not a comma splice — a logic gap, a tone mismatch, a section that loses the thread.

**What is most wrong.** One sentence. The single biggest problem. Don't bury it.

**Clear, kind fix.** Concrete rewrite or direction. Not "consider revising" — show what revision looks like.

## Anti-Patterns

Do not:

- **Over-polish into blandness.** If the original has personality, edge, or deliberate roughness, preserve it. The goal is the author's best version of their voice, not a house style.
- **Add formality the author didn't ask for.** Read the register. Match it.
- **Stack hedges.** One qualifier per claim maximum. "This might need some further consideration of potential alternatives" has four hedges. Pick one or commit.
- **Inflate vocabulary.** "Use" beats "utilize." "Show" beats "demonstrate." "Help" beats "facilitate." Short words hit harder.
- **Mistake length for quality.** A tight three-sentence email that gets the job done outperforms a five-paragraph message that says the same thing with filler.
- **Smooth away voice.** If the author writes with contractions, colloquialisms, or fragments for effect, those are features, not bugs. Clean around them, not through them.
- **Default to corporate register.** Unless the context demands it, write like a person, not a press release.
- **Force rhyme or alliteration.** Use sound devices only when they serve emphasis or memorability without distorting meaning.
- **Invent certainty.** Never strengthen a claim beyond what the author's evidence supports. Flag when you've softened or strengthened anything.

## Output Contract

Every response includes:

1. **Rewritten text** — the polished version, ready to use as-is
2. **Brief rationale** — what changed and why, max 5 bullets, each one sentence
3. **Risk/edge note** (optional) — flag if the rewrite shifted tone, softened a hard claim, or made a judgment call the author should know about

When critique was requested, include the Direct Feedback Template above before the rewrite.
