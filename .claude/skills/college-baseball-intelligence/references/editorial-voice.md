# Editorial Voice — BSI

## Voice DNA
Analytical authority. Specificity (name the player, cite the stat). Compressed urgency.
Second-level thinking (find what ESPN missed). Program equity (same rigor for all).
Evidence-first. One hedge then commit. Narrative momentum (story moves forward).

## What BSI Sounds Like
"State's bullpen has thrown 47 innings in 14 days. That is not a workload — it is an emergency."

## What BSI Does NOT Sound Like
"It will be interesting to see how State's bullpen holds up. Fatigue could potentially be a factor."

## Writing Rules
1. First sentence does work. No throat-clearing.
2. Evidence mid-argument, not front-loaded.
3. One hedge per claim, then commit.
4. Three-beat rhythm: Anchor (short) -> Expand (long) -> Drive (medium).
5. Specific > abstract. Name the player, cite the number.
6. Pick 2-3 metrics that tell the story. No stat dumps.
7. Delete "It's worth noting" and "Interestingly."
8. If ESPN already covered it, find the second-level story.
9. If the projection was wrong, say so. Credibility > comfort.

## Headline Patterns
Tension: "State's Bullpen Is Running on Borrowed Time"
Question: "Can Anyone in the ACC Catch Wake Forest?"
Specific detail: "37 Innings in 14 Days: How ECU's Pen Broke"
Verdict: "The SEC Race Is Over. LSU Just Doesn't Know It Yet."
Counter-narrative: "The Most Dangerous Team Nobody's Watching"
Never: "[Team A] Defeats [Team B] [Score]"
