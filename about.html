The Origin

Destiny Written Before Birth

My family knew I'd be born in Memphis, but they also knew I was meant for Texas. So they did something extraordinary: they collected soil from West Columbia—the first capital of the Republic of Texas, where Sam Houston was inaugurated as the first president.

That soil traveled from the birthplace of Texas independence to a Memphis hospital room. I was born on it. Literally on Texas soil.

The doctor looked at my parents, smiled, and said:

"You know you ain't the first to do this—but they've ALL been from Texas." — Delivering Physician, Memphis, TN

August 17, 1995—the same birthday as Davy Crockett, the Tennessee senator who left everything to defend Texas at the Alamo. Born in Tennessee, destined for Texas. History has a sense of poetry.

The Moment

November 27, 1998

Three years old. Darrell K Royal-Texas Memorial Stadium. Ricky Williams breaks the NCAA all-time rushing record against Texas A&M. Final score: Texas 26, A&M 24.

I was there, in a Texas t-shirt, watching history happen. Too young to understand statistics, old enough to feel the electricity when something legendary unfolds.

That game taught me what numbers can't capture: momentum, pressure, the intangibles that separate statistics from story. Twenty-five years later, I'm still trying to measure what I felt that day.

The Newspaper Article

"Tennessee birth will be on Texas soil"

The El Campo Leader-News ran the story on May 6, 1995 — three months before I was born. The article detailed how my family carried on a tradition spanning 127 years of Texans ensuring their children would be born on Texas soil, no matter where life took them.

My father, John Dean Humphrey, son of El Campo's Bill and Helen Humphrey, brought dirt from West Columbia — where Sam Houston was inaugurated as the first President of the Republic of Texas — to ensure that when I came into this world, my first cry would be on Texas soil.

"And just to be on the safe side, there will be the pearl of Texas, the seal of Texas, and a Lone Star emblazoned receiving blanket. And the 'Right Texas Stuff' for blastoff."

— El Campo Leader-News, May 6, 1995

Why Blaze Exists

The Problem

Analytics Platforms Built by People Who Never Competed

Most sports analytics tools are built by data scientists who optimize for statistical elegance, not competitive reality. They measure what's convenient—exit velocity, yards per play, shooting percentage—not what actually swings outcomes.

Coaches don't need prettier spreadsheets. They need answers: Should I pull this pitcher? Is this recruit worth the scholarship? What's the optimal play call on third-and-seven?

Blaze measures what matters. Situation. Momentum. Pressure. The stuff you feel at three years old in a stadium when records get broken.
